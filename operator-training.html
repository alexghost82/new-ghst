What Ghost Is
04 / 50
Before I touch anything — what exactly is Ghost?
Doctrine · Lesson 1

Ghost understands what your cameras see. It does not just detect things in front of them.

A conventional monitoring station shows you pixels and leaves the understanding to you. Ghost inverts that. Every camera becomes a conversation partner: you ask, in your own words, and Ghost answers about what is happening, what happened before, and what deviates from the instructions you gave it.

That distinction — understanding versus detecting — is the foundation of everything in this course. Ghost does not hand you a box labeled with an object class. It reads the scene: a delivery truck idling against the flow of the loading dock during shift change is a different fact than the same truck parked there at 03:00 with its lights off.

  • Past — a memory you can question
    "Did anyone approach the server-room door after the cleaning crew left?" Ghost answers from what it saw, with timestamps.
  • Present — eyes on the site right now
    "Is the fire lane by Gate 2 clear for the ambulance?" Ghost samples the live feed and answers in seconds.
  • Future — standing instructions
    "From now on, tell me if a vehicle stops on the perimeter road for more than two minutes." Ghost keeps the watch and interrupts you only on deviation.

The Operator's Role
05 / 50
If Ghost does the watching, what is my job?
Doctrine · Lesson 2

Ghost keeps the watch. You command it.

Your job shifts from staring at screens to directing an intelligence asset. You decide what matters on this site, encode it as questions, standing instructions, and alert rules — and judge what comes back. Ghost is tireless and literal; you supply context, priorities, and decisions.

  • You define what "normal" means
    Every site has its own rhythm. The night-shift forklift that is routine at a 24/7 depot is an anomaly at a sealed pharmaceutical warehouse. You teach Ghost the difference.
  • You ask the second question
    Ghost's first answer is rarely the end. "A maintenance contractor entered the electrical room" should trigger your follow-up: "Was he carrying anything when he left?"
  • You own every alert
    When Ghost interrupts, a human acknowledges, verifies on the live feed, and decides. Ghost never closes its own alerts — that is deliberate.
  • You keep the system honest
    Rules drift, cameras move, sites change. A professional operator reviews standing instructions at the start of every shift.
Mindset check
Write your site's three questions
Before the next lesson, write down the three questions you most wish your cameras could answer about your site. You will ask Ghost all three by the end of Part III.

First Entry: Secure Access
07 / 50
How do I actually sign in to the console?
Orientation · Lesson 4

Two doors in. Credentials, or a quick-login link.

Ghost's console is reached through the Secure Access screen. You will use one of two paths, depending on how your site administrator provisioned you:

  1. Agent credentials
    Enter your agent name and your Ghost API key on the Secure Access screen, then press Sign in. The eye icon next to the key field toggles its visibility — keep it hidden whenever anyone else can see your screen.
  2. Quick-login link
    An administrator can issue you a one-time magic link. Opening it signs you straight into the console — no typing. Links are single-use and expire; treat an unused one like a key to the building.
  3. Verify where you landed
    After sign-in you should see the conversations sidebar on one side and an empty chat inviting you to start. If you see the marketing site instead, your session expired — sign in again.
Session discipline
Sessions expire on schedule. Ghost returns you to Secure Access — never leave a signed-in console unattended.

Your First Conversation
08 / 50
Walk me through starting my very first conversation.
Conversation Core · Lesson 1

One conversation per camera, per concern. That is the whole data model.

Everything in Ghost lives inside conversations. A conversation typically represents one camera and one ongoing concern — "Loading Dock — North", "Pharmacy Cold Storage", "Perimeter Road East". You will open many; each keeps its own history, its own cameras, its own instructions, and its own alert rules.

  1. Press the + button at the top of the sidebar
    A fresh conversation appears, stamped with the date and time. It is selected automatically and the message box is focused, ready to type.
  2. Ask something — even without a camera
    Type a question and press Enter. Without a camera attached, Ghost converses from knowledge and from this conversation's history. Try: "What should I include in a handover summary for a night shift?"
  3. Watch the answer stream
    Ghost replies token by token. While it streams, the message box waits — one question, one answer, in order.
Field drill 1

Asking Good Questions
09 / 50
How do I phrase questions so Ghost gives me operational answers?
Conversation Core · Lesson 2

Ask about the situation, not about objects.

Ghost reads scenes the way a briefing officer would. The more operational intent your question carries, the sharper the answer. A question that names the concern — safety, security, operations, emergency — tells Ghost what matters in the frame and what to ignore.

Ask like this
  • "Is the forklift blocking the fire lane at the loading dock?"
  • "Did the fuel tanker finish unloading, and were cones placed around it?"
  • "Is anyone working at height on the scaffold without a harness?"
  • "Has the gate been left open since the last delivery?"
Not like this
  • "Is there a person in the frame?"
  • "Do you see a car?"
  • "Detect objects."
  • "What's in the picture?"

The left column gets you decisions; the right column gets you inventory. When an answer feels generic, your question was generic — add the operational context and ask again. Follow-ups inherit the conversation's context, so "And now?" or "Has that changed?" work exactly as they would with a human colleague.

The Composer in Depth
10 / 50
What does every control around the message box do?
Conversation Core · Lesson 3

The composer is your command line. Four controls, total mastery required.

  • The message box
    Type and press Enter to send; Shift+Enter inserts a line break. While Ghost is answering, the box shows "waiting for reply" and pauses input — answers always arrive in order.
  • Live camera toggle
    The camera button beside the box. When live is on, every question you send is accompanied by freshly sampled frames from the connected cameras — Ghost answers about right now. When off, Ghost answers from history and knowledge only. This toggle is the single most important switch in the console.
  • Microphone
    When voice command is enabled in Settings, the mic button listens and types for you. Saying your send phrase (default: "go ghost") fires the message hands-free — invaluable when you are holding a radio.
  • Send button
    The arrow sends, same as Enter. If it is dimmed, either the box is empty or Ghost is still answering.

Above the box, a thin strip reports what is attached to your next message: which cameras are live, and whether voice is listening. Read that strip before you send — it is the difference between asking about the live scene and asking about the past.

Same question, two modes
Ask the same question once with live off and once with live on (after Part IV teaches you to connect a camera, repeat this). Pass condition: you can explain the difference between the two answers.

Reading the Thread
11 / 50
What should I notice inside Ghost's answers?
Conversation Core · Lesson 4

Every answer carries evidence. Learn to read all of it.

The thread is more than text. Professional operators extract four layers from every exchange:

  • The sampled frame
    When Ghost answered from a live camera, the answer carries a thumbnail of the exact frames it analyzed — watermarked with time and camera label. Click it to open a full-size preview; Esc closes. This is your evidence trail: the answer and what Ghost saw, side by side.
  • Timestamps on hover
    Hover any message to reveal its exact local time. In an investigation, "when was this asked" matters as much as the answer.
  • Copy controls
    Hovering your own messages reveals a copy button; code or structured blocks in Ghost's answers carry their own. Use them when filing reports — never retype evidence.
  • Multi-camera attribution
    When several cameras are live, Ghost answers per camera and labels each reply with the camera it describes. Never assume — read the label.

If you scroll up through history, a "jump to latest" button appears — one click returns you to the live end of the thread. During fast-moving events, stay at the bottom; the thread auto-follows new answers.

Managing Conversations
12 / 50
How do I keep dozens of conversations under control?
Conversation Core · Lesson 5

Name things like a professional. Future-you is the customer.

Hover any conversation in the sidebar and an action bar appears. These small controls are how a fifty-conversation site stays navigable:

  • Rename (pencil, or double-click the title)
    Give every long-lived conversation a name a stranger could navigate by: "Gate 2 — Vehicle Inspection Lane", not "camera test". Renaming locks the title against automatic changes.
  • Automatic naming (sparkles)
    By default, Ghost names new conversations from their content after the first exchanges. The sparkles button overrides this per conversation; the global switch lives in Settings → Response Tuning.
  • Delete (trash)
    Removes the conversation and its history immediately — there is no confirmation and no undo. Treat it like shredding a file: deliberate, never casual.
  • Status badges
    Each row shows what matters at a glance: a shield when alert mode is armed, a camera count when cameras are saved, a live badge when streaming, and the time of last activity.

The System Prompt
13 / 50
How do I give a conversation standing instructions?
Conversation Core · Lesson 6

The system prompt is your standing order. Ghost obeys it in every single answer.

Open it from the sliders icon in the conversation header. Whatever you write there shapes every answer in this conversation — tone, focus, format, and priorities. It is the most underused control in the console, and the one that most separates professionals from beginners.

system-prompt · example · loading dock
"You are watching the north loading dock of a pharmaceutical warehouse. Priorities, in order: (1) the fire lane must stay clear, (2) cold-chain pallets must not wait outside the chiller for more than 10 minutes, (3) every truck at the dock must have its wheels chocked. Answer in two sentences or fewer. When you flag a problem, name the bay number."
  • Be concrete and ordered
    Numbered priorities beat prose. Ghost applies them in order when a scene contains several issues at once.
  • Dictate the format you need
    "Two sentences or fewer", "always include the bay number", "answer in Hebrew" — format instructions hold across the entire conversation.
  • Revisit on every site change
    New tenant, new traffic pattern, construction starting — the prompt must follow reality. A stale standing order is worse than none.
Field drill 4
Write one standing order, prove it holds
Set a system prompt that forces a two-sentence answer format, then ask three different questions. Pass condition: all three answers obey the format without being reminded.

Response Tuning
14 / 50
Can I trade speed for accuracy per conversation?
Conversation Core · Lesson 7

Every conversation has its own dials. Tune them to the mission.

Settings → Response Tuning exposes per-conversation dials. They apply to the active conversation, so a perimeter watch and an investigation desk can run completely different profiles.

Accuracy level · 1–4
Left is fastest, right is most thorough. Routine status checks run at 1–2; evidence-grade analysis of a complex scene deserves 3–4.
Response length
Short, medium, or long. Live operations want short — you can always ask for elaboration. Shift-end summaries want long.
Image detail
How closely Ghost examines sampled frames. High resolves badge text and license plates at distance; low answers faster on busy scenes.
Capture quality
How sharply this browser samples camera frames before sending. Sharp by default; drop to fast on weak hardware or thin networks.

Recommended profiles: Perimeter watch — accuracy 2, short answers, low detail, fast capture: cadence beats nuance. Investigation — accuracy 4, long answers, high detail, sharp capture: nuance beats cadence. Set the profile when you create the conversation, and you will never think about it mid-shift.

Connecting Your First Camera
15 / 50
How do I attach a camera to a conversation?
Cameras · Lesson 1

A conversation with a camera attached is a camera you can question.

  1. Open the camera selector
    In the conversation header, press "Add camera" — or flip the composer's live toggle when no camera is set up yet. Ghost lists every camera the console can reach. Grant the browser camera permission the first time.
  2. Select one or more cameras
    Click rows to select. A conversation can carry several cameras — a gate conversation often pairs the approach view with the barrier view.
  3. Save Setup — or just Enable Live
    "Save setup" binds the cameras to this conversation permanently and starts streaming. "Enable live" streams for this session only, without saving. For any conversation you intend to keep, save.
  4. Confirm in the header
    Saved cameras appear as labeled chips in the conversation header. The small × on each chip detaches it. The live stage (next lesson) appears above the composer.

Saved Setup vs Live Session
16 / 50
What's the difference between saving a camera and just going live?
Cameras · Lesson 2

Saved is the contract. Live is the moment.

This distinction trips up every new operator, so commit it to memory:

Saved setup
Persistent. Lives with the conversation, survives sign-out and browser restarts, appears as chips in the header. Standing alerts and area-wide broadcasts use the saved setup as their source of truth.
Live session
Momentary. The composer toggle starts and stops streaming right now, in this browser. Turning live off does not unsave anything; turning it on without a setup opens the selector.

The professional pattern: save the correct camera set once, when you create the conversation — then use the live toggle freely throughout the shift. Stream when you are actively questioning; rest the toggle when you are not. Saved bindings quietly carry the rest of the system: when Part VI arms a standing alert, it watches the saved cameras; when Part V broadcasts to an area, it collects every saved camera inside it.

  • Removing a saved camera
    Press the × on its header chip. It detaches from the conversation and drops out of any live session at once.
  • Re-checking the binding
    Camera moved or replaced? Open the selector again and re-save. A conversation bound to yesterday's camera angle answers about the wrong scene with full confidence — re-save after every physical change.

The Live Stage
17 / 50
What is the video panel above the message box?
Cameras · Lesson 3

The live stage is your eyes. The conversation is your judgment.

Whenever cameras are streaming, the live stage renders above the composer — one tile per camera, side by side when several are live. It exists so you never answer "did Ghost get it right?" from memory: the scene and the conversation share one screen.

  • Collapse / expand
    The chevron shrinks the stage to a slim bar when you need thread space, and restores it on click. The setting is global — it follows you across conversations.
  • Resize handles
    Drag the bottom corners to size the stage to your monitor. Ghost remembers your preferred size.
  • Connection states
    A tile shows "connecting" while a stream warms up and an explicit error if the camera cannot be reached. A black tile is a fact to act on — Part X's troubleshooting guide covers the causes.
  • Scene overlays
    The stage can silhouette people and vehicles in the frame as a quick orientation aid. Overlays pause automatically while alert mode is armed — the watch always takes priority over decoration.

Discipline note: the stage tempts new operators back into screen-staring. Resist it. Glance to verify; question to understand. Your throughput as an operator comes from the conversation, not the tiles.

Enhance: Zoom, Pan & Views
18 / 50
Can I look closer at part of the frame?
Cameras · Lesson 4

Two ways of looking: the intel view and the enhanced view.

Each live tile carries an enhance control that switches between two operator views:

Intel view (default)
A calm, grayscale rendering of the full frame. Built for long watches — low fatigue, full situational coverage, nothing hidden.
Enhanced view
Full color with zoom and pan unlocked. Built for moments: reading a container number, checking whether the contractor's badge is clipped on, confirming what a driver placed by the fence.
  • Zoom — wheel or buttons
    Scroll the mouse wheel over the tile, or use the + / − controls. Up to 8× magnification in smooth steps.
  • Pan — drag
    Once zoomed past 1×, drag the image to move around the frame. The cursor switches to a grab hand.
  • Reset
    One press returns to 1×, centered. Always reset before handing the console over — the next operator must see the whole scene.

Critical detail: while a tile is enhanced and zoomed, the frames Ghost samples for your questions match what you are looking at. Frame the subject, then ask — "Read the placard on this trailer" works because Ghost sees your framing.

Recording & Clips
19 / 50
How do I record what a camera is showing right now?
Cameras · Lesson 5

When something is happening, record first and keep questioning.

Every live tile has a record control. Recording captures the tile's stream into a clip you can download — your raw-footage layer for evidence and handover, running parallel to Ghost's written answers.

  1. Start
    Press record on the tile. A timer counts the take. If the camera carries audio and listening is on, audio is captured too.
  2. Stop
    Press again. The finished clip appears beside the tile, stamped with duration.
  3. Download or discard
    Download saves the clip file to the console machine; delete discards it. Clips live in this browser session — anything worth keeping must be downloaded before you sign out.
Field drill 6
The evidence sandwich
Start recording, ask Ghost two questions about the live scene, stop recording, download the clip. Pass condition: you hold a clip file plus the thread's written answers covering the same minute — a complete evidence package.

Doctrine: recording complements the conversation, it does not replace it. A clip shows what happened; the thread shows what it meant and when you knew it. Investigations want both.

Can I listen to a camera as well as watch it?
Cameras · Lesson 6

Some incidents are heard before they are seen.

Cameras that carry a microphone expose a listen control on their live tile. One press opens the audio channel through your console; press again to mute. Cameras without a microphone show the control as unavailable — that is the hardware, not a fault.

  • When to listen
    Verification moments: raised voices at a service counter, an alarm tone somewhere off-frame, machinery that sounds wrong before it looks wrong, glass breaking out of view.
  • When to stay muted
    Default state. Continuous audio fatigues you faster than continuous video, and a muted channel cannot mask the radio. Open audio deliberately, close it when verified.
  • Audio in recordings
    If listening is on while you record, the clip includes the audio track — note it in your incident log, and know your site's policy on audio capture before relying on it.

Privacy discipline applies double to audio. Many jurisdictions and most site policies treat sound differently from video. Your site lead will brief you on local rules — until then, treat audio as a verification tool, not a surveillance channel.

Writing Good Watch Rules
28 / 50
How do I write rules that catch real events and ignore noise?
Standing Alerts · Lesson 2

A good rule reads like an order to a guard: subject, condition, context.

Open the alert panel (shield icon), type a rule into the rule box, press Enter or the + to add it. A conversation can hold several rules; each has its own on/off toggle, and hover reveals a delete control. The craft is in the wording:

Rules that work
  • "A person climbs or reaches over the perimeter fence"
  • "A vehicle stops in the fire lane for more than a moment"
  • "Someone enters the chemical store without a hi-vis vest"
  • "The loading bay door is open with no staff present"
  • "A person raises a hand high above their head"
Rules that misfire
  • "Anything suspicious" — no observable condition
  • "A person appears" — fires on every employee
  • "Theft" — a legal conclusion, not a visible event
  • "Motion" — that is noise, not a rule
  • Describe what the camera can see
    Posture, position, clothing, equipment, door states, vehicle behavior. If a human watching the feed could not call it, neither can the rule.
  • One deviation per rule
    Split "open door or missing vest or loitering" into three rules. You get cleaner alerts and can toggle each watch independently.

When the Alarm Fires
31 / 50
An alert just fired. What is the procedure?
Standing Alerts · Lesson 5

Mute. Verify. Acknowledge. In that order, every time.

A firing alert takes the whole screen: a full-screen overlay with a strobe border, an audible siren, the matched rule, Ghost's description of what it saw, and the captured frame. It is designed to be impossible to miss. Your procedure:

  1. Mute the siren
    Press the mute control or the M key. Silence buys you focus; the overlay stays until you act on it.
  2. Verify against the evidence
    Read the matched rule, read Ghost's description, study the captured frame. Is this the deviation the rule was written for? You have the live feed one click away if you need the present tense.
  3. Go to the conversation — or acknowledge
    "Go to conversation" jumps you into the alerting camera's thread with the alert recorded in it, ready for follow-up questions: "Is the person still there? Which way did they go?" Acknowledge (Enter or Esc) closes the overlay and logs you as the human who took it.
  4. Act per your site's escalation order
    Ghost ends at the decision boundary. Radio, dispatch, lockdown calls — those belong to your standing orders. The thread you just built is your timeline when you write it up.

Acknowledgment is accountability: every alert records who cleared it. Never acknowledge an alarm you have not looked at — clearing blind is the cardinal sin of this profession.

36 / 50
When and how do I close a case properly?
Incidents · Lesson 4

A case is closed when the story is complete — not when the noise stops.

Closure is a deliberate act with its own dialog: a resolution classification and a closing summary, stamped into the timeline forever. Before you close, run the three-question audit:

  1. Is the cause established?
    "Contractor entered the wrong door — badge valid, signage missing" closes. "It stopped happening" does not; that case is resolved-unexplained and your handover should say so.
  2. Is the action recorded?
    Whatever was done — patrol dispatched, door secured, rule reworded, no action warranted — must appear in notes with a time. An empty timeline plus a closed status is an audit finding waiting to happen.
  3. Did the system learn?
    Every false alarm is a rule asking to be sharpened, and every real catch may justify widening a watch. Feed the lesson back into Part VI before you close the tab.

The board's KPIs aggregate from exactly these habits: time-to-acknowledge, time-to-close, open-case age. They are not surveillance of you — they are the site's early-warning system for understaffed shifts and noisy rules. Keep the data honest by keeping the casework honest.

Heads-up displays come and go; the incident archive is the institution's memory. Five years from now, the only account of tonight will be what you wrote in that workspace.

Put it all together — what does a professional shift look like?
The Craft · Lesson 1

Excellence is a checklist run so often it disappears.

  1. Take over deliberately (first 10 minutes)
    Switch the console to your user. Read the outgoing operator's handover. Check every armed post's shield dot and status card. Run the incident board's badge to zero with triage. Then broadcast-sweep each area: "anything abnormal, unsafe, or out of place?"
  2. Run the watch (the long middle)
    Work by exception: standing alerts interrupt, broadcast sweeps on cadence fill the gaps. Verify every alarm against its frame before acknowledging. Note actions in the incident workspace as they happen — never "later".
  3. Investigate in the conversation, not in your head
    Every hunch becomes a question to the relevant camera. The thread is your worknotes — if you wondered it, ask it, so the record shows what was checked.
  4. Hand over in writing (last 15 minutes)
    Final sweep per area. In each critical conversation, ask for a long-format shift summary. Open incidents get a status note. Disarmed posts get a reason and a re-arm time. Your relief should need five minutes, not fifty.

The routine is the floor, not the ceiling: it guarantees a competent shift on your worst day and frees your attention for judgment on the days that demand it.

Troubleshooting Field Guide
47 / 50
Something's not working. Give me the field guide.
The Craft · Lesson 2

Diagnose in order: camera, channel, rule, question.

  • Black or "connecting" tile
    Camera permission, cable, or device. Re-open the camera selector and re-pick the device; if it lists nothing, the browser lost camera permission or the camera lost power. Physical check beats refresh.
  • Answers ignore the live scene
    The live toggle is off, or no cameras are saved. Check the strip above the composer — it tells you exactly what your next message carries.
  • Alert never fires
    In order: is the conversation armed (shield dot)? Is the specific rule toggled active? Does the rule describe something visible on this camera's actual view? Run Site Intelligence and read what Ghost can see — then re-word the rule in those terms.
  • Alert fires constantly
    The rule names a normal condition. Add the distinguishing context: not "a vehicle at the gate" but "a vehicle waiting at the gate with no guard present for more than a minute".
  • Yellow shield dot
    Armed but degraded — usually the push channel. Check the panel's status card; the channel reconnects on its own, but verify "last scan" is still advancing.
  • Vague answers
    Usually a vague question — add the operational concern. Then check the dials: accuracy and image detail may be tuned for speed on a conversation that now needs precision.
  • Session bounced to Secure Access
    Sessions expire by design. Sign back in, or keep a quick-login link from Part IX ready for exactly this moment.

Escalate to your site administrator anything the guide does not clear in ten minutes — with the conversation name, the time, and what you already ruled out.

  • Proportionality
    Ghost watches for deviations you define. Define them for safety, security, and operations — never for curiosity about individuals. Your rules are an ethical document; write them so you would be comfortable reading them aloud.
